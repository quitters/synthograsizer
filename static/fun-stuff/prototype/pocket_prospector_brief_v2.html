Concept Overview

Pocket Prospector transplants the scratch-ticket interaction model from Scritchy Scratchy into a mining fantasy, while adopting Cookie Clicker's transparent progression economy. The player scratches through procedurally generated rock tiles to uncover minerals, gems, and fossils. Each "claim" is a unique tile grid with its own geology, reward table, and visual theme — the equivalent of a themed scratch ticket.

Where Cookie Clicker asks "how big is my number?", Pocket Prospector asks "what will I find next?" The process-focused engagement loop (the scratch) is the star; the outcome-focused loop (currency accumulation and reinvestment) provides long-term structure and prestige motivation.

Core Loop

Scratch rock tiles → uncover minerals → sell for gold → upgrade tools → unlock deeper claims → prestige to reset with permanent bonuses → repeat with new geology and deeper strata.

Design Pillars Philosophy

Process over Outcome

Every scratch must feel good. Variable reward, progressive disclosure, and tactile audio feedback make the dig itself the primary source of satisfaction — not just the number it produces.

Layered Information

Surface play is visual and intuitive (scratch, see colors, collect). Optimization play reveals probability tables, hardness stats, and efficiency calculations — available on demand, never forced.

Catalog as Progression

New claim types unlock in themed catalogs (Shallow Soil, Deep Stone, Volcanic Core, Fossil Beds). Progression feels like discovery and collection, not a linear store scroll.

Modular Architecture

Every system — claims, minerals, upgrades, prestige — is a self-contained module with a clean interface. New content ships as data, not code changes.

Negative Design Space New in v2

Design is as much about what you exclude as what you include. Pocket Prospector deliberately rejects specific patterns from both inspiration games.

Rejected from Scritchy Scratchy

Desk clutter as atmosphere. Scritchy Scratchy scatters 15+ tickets across the desk with overlapping decorative objects. This creates charm but harms scannability. Pocket Prospector's workbench displays one active claim at a time, with completed specimens organized in jars — not scattered. Atmosphere comes from lighting, sound, and surface texture, not from visual noise.

Kept from Scritchy Scratchy

Skeuomorphic workspace with themed objects. The workbench, lantern, field journal, and specimen jars establish place without cluttering the interaction surface. Each decorative element has a gameplay function.

Rejected from Cookie Clicker

Single-verb monotony. Cookie Clicker's click never changes — click one and click one billion are identical. Pocket Prospector's scratching verb evolves with tool upgrades: wider brush, deeper penetration, different particle effects. The action stays the same; the feel scales with progression.

Kept from Cookie Clicker

Always-visible economy sidebar. Currency, production rate, and upgrade costs never require a submenu to find. The player can always answer "what should I buy next?" at a glance.

Claim Identity & Feel New in v2

The essay argued that Scritchy Scratchy's per-ticket theming (Berry Picking's tree shape, worms as negative symbols) is what makes each scratch unique. Pocket Prospector must achieve the same: every claim type must feel different, not just pay different amounts. This table defines the experiential axes that differentiate claims.

Hardness as Player-Facing Mechanic

Hardness is not just a data field — it directly shapes the scratching experience. Each tile has a hardness value that determines how many scratch strokes are needed to reveal it. Low-hardness tiles (Shallow Soil) crumble in one or two swipes, creating a fast and breezy rhythm. High-hardness tiles (Volcanic Core) require multiple deliberate strokes, each one chipping away a percentage of the tile's opacity and producing progressively more dramatic particle effects as the mineral beneath begins to show through. Tool power upgrades reduce the effective hardness, making previously laborious claims feel effortless — a tangible embodiment of progression that the player feels rather than just reads in a stat.

Idle & Offline Mechanics New in v2

An incremental game that only rewards active play isn't truly incremental. Pocket Prospector must answer the question: what happens when the player closes the tab?

Passive Mining Drones

Unlocked after the first prestige, mining drones are the primary idle mechanic. Each drone is assigned to a claim type and slowly auto-scratches tiles while the player is away. Drones are significantly less efficient than manual play: they ignore multipliers from scratch combos, never trigger bonus events, and operate at roughly 20% of manual gold-per-second. This preserves the process-focused philosophy — active scratching is always more rewarding — while ensuring players who return after hours or days have meaningful progress to collect.

Offline Calculation

On return, the game calculates offline earnings based on drone assignments, elapsed time (capped at 8 hours to prevent absurd accumulation), and a "mine stability" multiplier that degrades over time. The player is shown a "mining report" — a skeuomorphic clipboard displaying what each drone found — which itself becomes a small moment of discovery: "what did I miss while I was gone?"

Idle Upgrades

The upgrade tree includes drone-specific improvements: drone speed, drone luck (chance to find rares offline), and drone slots (more simultaneous claims). These are balanced to be meaningful but never superior to investing in manual scratch upgrades, maintaining the active/idle hierarchy.

Prestige: The Deeper Dig New in v2

The prestige system must answer an emotional question, not just a mechanical one: why would I give up everything I've earned?

The Pitch

Each prestige represents digging deeper into the earth. Your first run scratches the surface — Shallow Soil and early Stone. When you prestige, you reset your gold and upgrades but unlock the next stratum. The workbench visually shifts: the lantern glows dimmer, the rock on the bench darkens, the ambient sound deepens. You aren't resetting — you're descending. The game's fiction makes the reset feel like progress, not loss.

What Carries Over

Prestige Picks — a permanent currency earned from lifetime gold at reset, spent on a separate upgrade tree that includes global multipliers, drone unlocks, and cosmetic workbench items. The Field Journal — every mineral, fossil, and gem you've ever found stays recorded, building a permanent collection that persists across all resets. Catalog Access — once a catalog tier is unlocked, it remains unlocked forever.

What Changes

Each stratum introduces new claim types, new hazards, and new visual themes. Stratum 2 (Deep Stone) adds gas pockets and crystal veins. Stratum 3 (Volcanic) adds lava timers and obsidian. Stratum 4 (Fossil Beds) adds the fragility mechanic. The prestige loop gives each run a distinct character — not just bigger numbers, but genuinely new things to scratch.

Onboarding: First Five Minutes New in v2

The essay criticized Scritchy Scratchy for making it hard for new players to distinguish interactive elements from decorative ones. Pocket Prospector solves this through a structured first-run experience that teaches by doing, not by explaining.

The First Claim

Player sees a single rock slab on an otherwise empty bench. Only the scratch cursor is active. No sidebar, no upgrades, no journal. The only possible action is to scratch. The player learns the core verb in isolation.

The First Sale

After uncovering the first mineral, a "Sell" prompt appears. Gold counter fades in. The player learns currency exists and that minerals convert to it. The sidebar slides in with the first affordable upgrade.

The First Upgrade

After purchasing their first tool upgrade, the next claim arrives. The scratch now feels different — faster, wider. The player discovers that upgrades have tactile consequences, not just numerical ones.

The Full Bench

After 3 claims, the field journal, specimen jars, and catalog panel fade in. Each element highlights briefly on first appearance. The workbench is now complete, but the player has been introduced to each element individually.

This progressive disclosure mirrors the essay's observation that Scritchy Scratchy's layered information architecture is a strength — but only if the layers are introduced in order, not dumped simultaneously.

Systems Architecture Technical

The codebase is organized as a modular event-driven system. Each module registers with a central EventBus and communicates through typed events. No module directly imports another — all coupling flows through events and a shared state store.

ClaimEngine

Generates tile grids from geology templates. Handles scratch input, hit detection, hardness calculation, and reveal animation triggers.

EconomyManager

Tracks currencies (gold, gems, fossils). Processes transactions, validates purchases, and emits balance-change events for UI binding.

UpgradeRegistry

Defines upgrade trees (tool power, scratch radius, luck multiplier, drone stats). Each upgrade is a data object with cost curve, effect function, and max level.

CatalogSystem

Manages claim-type unlocks, catalog theming, and collection tracking. Gates content behind prestige tiers and discovery milestones.

PrestigeLoop

Handles soft resets. Calculates Prestige Picks from lifetime earnings, applies permanent multipliers, unlocks new strata, and triggers the visual "descent" transition.

SaveManager

Serializes state to localStorage with versioned JSON schema. Handles forward migration via version-keyed transform functions. Export/import as base64 for manual backup.

IdleEngine

Manages drone assignments, offline time calculation (capped at 8h), mine stability decay, and the mining report generation on return.

InputRouter

Normalizes mouse and touch input into a unified scratch stream. Handles multi-touch rejection, palm filtering, scroll-vs-scratch disambiguation, and pressure sensitivity on supported devices.

State Management

The shared state store is a reactive proxy-based object. Each top-level key (economy, upgrades, catalog, prestige, idle) is owned by its respective module. Modules write to their own slice and read from others. The proxy traps set operations and emits granular change events on the EventBus, allowing UI components to subscribe to specific paths (e.g., economy.gold) without polling or full-state diffing. This replaces a heavier framework dependency while providing the reactivity needed for live-updating sidebar panels.

Rendering Architecture

The application uses a split rendering model: the central workbench (claim grid, scratch interaction, particle effects) is rendered on a Canvas element for performance; the peripheral UI (sidebar panels, upgrade lists, catalog browser, field journal) is standard HTML/CSS with DOM updates driven by EventBus subscriptions. A thin compositing layer manages z-order between the Canvas and overlaid DOM elements (tooltips, the mining report modal). This avoids the complexity of a full Canvas UI while keeping the performance-critical scratch loop off the DOM.

// Module registration pattern
class ClaimEngine extends GameModule {
  init(bus, store) {
    bus.on('claim:scratch', ({ pos, pressure }) => {
      const damage = this.calcDamage(pressure, store.upgrades.power);
      const tile = this.applyDamage(pos, damage);
      if (tile.revealed) bus.emit('mineral:found', tile.contents);
    });
  }
}

Testing Strategy

Each module is independently testable because it depends only on an EventBus instance and a state slice — both trivially mockable. The test harness creates a sandboxed bus/store pair, registers the module under test, emits input events, and asserts on output events and state mutations. Claim geology tables are validated at load time (probabilities sum to ≤ 1.0, valueMult is numeric, hardness is positive integer). Integration tests replay recorded scratch sequences against the full module graph and assert on final economy state.

UX Principles Design

From Scritchy Scratchy: Atmosphere & Process

The play area is a miner's workbench — a skeuomorphic surface with a lantern, field journal, and specimen jars. Claims sit on the bench as physical rock slabs. The environment is decorative but functional: the field journal tracks collection progress, the specimen jars show recent finds, and the lantern brightness reflects current luck multiplier. Unlike Scritchy Scratchy's cluttered desk, every object on the workbench has a gameplay role — nothing is purely decorative.

From Cookie Clicker: Transparency & Legibility

Currency, production rate, and upgrade costs are always visible in fixed sidebar panels — never hidden behind hover states for core metrics. The upgrade panel uses Cookie Clicker's linear-list clarity: name, cost, level, and effect are all visible at a glance. The player can always answer "what should I buy next?" without opening a submenu.

Layered Detail on Demand

Core metrics (gold, upgrade costs, claim level) are always visible in the sidebar. Advanced statistics (geology probability tables, expected value per scratch, hardness breakdowns, drone efficiency rates) are accessible through the field journal — a tabbed interface that opens over the workbench on click. This mirrors the Berry Picking detail panel from Scritchy Scratchy: the information exists for optimizers, but the casual player never encounters it unless they look.

Synthesis: Best of Both

The central workspace is atmospheric and tactile (Scritchy Scratchy's philosophy). The peripheral UI is transparent and information-dense (Cookie Clicker's philosophy). The boundary between these zones is sharp and consistent: workbench = experience, sidebars = information. This spatial contract lets players shift between process-focused and outcome-focused play by moving their eyes, not navigating menus.

Mobile & Touch Input New in v2

Scratching on a touchscreen is not a mouse interaction with a different pointer — it is a fundamentally different physical gesture with its own design challenges.

Scratch vs. Scroll

The primary conflict on mobile is disambiguating a scratch gesture from a page scroll. Solution: the workbench Canvas consumes all touch events within its bounds. Scrolling the page is handled by the sidebar panels only. The workbench fills the viewport on mobile (sidebars collapse to bottom-sheet drawers), eliminating the ambiguity entirely — if you're touching the main area, you're scratching.

Multi-Touch & Palm Rejection

The InputRouter accepts only single-point touch input for scratching. Additional touch points are ignored (not averaged). Palm rejection uses a simple area heuristic: touch events with a radiusX or radiusY above a threshold are discarded. This is imperfect but handles the majority of accidental palm contacts on tablets.

Responsive Layout

On screens below 700px, the layout shifts from side-by-side (workbench + sidebar) to stacked (workbench full-width, sidebar as a swipe-up bottom sheet). Upgrade purchases happen in the bottom sheet; the workbench is never obscured during scratching. The field journal opens as a full-screen overlay. This preserves the spatial contract (workbench = experience, panels = information) in a vertical form factor.

Evaluation Criteria Grading

Modularity (25%)

Can a new claim type be added by writing a single data object? Can a new upgrade be registered without modifying existing modules? Is each module independently testable with a mocked EventBus and state slice? The architecture should pass the "new content = new data, not new code" test.

Design Philosophy Integration (25%)

Does the game demonstrate understanding of both Scritchy Scratchy's process-focused UX and Cookie Clicker's outcome-focused transparency? Are the lessons from the essay visibly applied? Is the negative design space — what was deliberately excluded — evident in the final product?

Player Experience (25%)

Is the scratch satisfying? Do different claim types feel experientially distinct? Is the onboarding clear without being patronizing? Does prestige feel like descent rather than loss? Does the first minute hook and the first hour sustain? Balance, feel, and juice matter here.

Technical Quality (25%)

Clean code, consistent patterns, no global state leaks, performant Canvas rendering, split rendering model properly composited, save system resilience with migration support, touch input handled correctly. The codebase should read as well as it plays.
